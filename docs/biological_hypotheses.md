# Biological Hypotheses

These hypotheses guide candidate-panel construction and future analyses. They are not conclusions.

| Mechanism | Candidate genes/pathways | Expected evidence | Major confounders |
|---|---|---|---|
| DNA repair and genome stability | `TP53`, `ERCC1`, `ERCC4`, `RAD51`, `BRCA1`, `MSH2`, checkpoint genes | Orthology-supported intact genes, domain integrity, reproducible copy-number or sequence features across resources, retina expression when relevant | Annotation absence, isoforms, duplicated fragments, assembly collapse/expansion, deep-sea ecology |
| p53 pathway | `TP53`, `MDM2`, `ATM`, `CHEK2`, apoptosis and checkpoint genes | Conserved domains and carefully validated sequence features | Inference from sequence alone cannot establish pathway activity |
| Chromatin and histones | `H1F0`/linker histone H1.0, chromatin remodelers, heterochromatin regulators | Orthology and domain/region support; careful reproduction of published alignment or selection signals before interpretation | Broad conservation, paralogy, tissue specificity, model sensitivity |
| Telomere biology | `TERT`, `TERF1`, `TERF2`, `POT1`, `TINF2`, `ACD`/`TPP1`, `TERF2IP`, future `TERC` if assessable | Presence/copy-number with orthology and domain checks; telomeric motif scaffold-end enrichment as sequence context | Telomeric motif repeats do not imply telomere length, telomerase activity, rejuvenation, or longevity mechanism |
| Repeat and transposon context | Candidate loci near repeat annotations, especially ferritin/linker-histone/DNA-repair loci | Existing or externally generated repeat annotations intersected with candidate loci and flanking windows; artifact/context evidence only | Repeat overlap does not imply function, validated duplication, adaptation, or causation; repeat annotations can be incomplete or class-limited |
| Ferroptosis, iron handling, oxidative stress | Ferritins including `FTH1`/`FTH1B`, `GPX4`, glutathione genes, `NFE2L2`, `KEAP1` | Orthology-supported presence/copy-number and expression evidence where design allows | Diet, tissue, environment, and redox state cannot be inferred from genome alone; ferritin copy number is artifact-prone without locus validation |
| Mitochondrial biology and metabolism | OXPHOS, mitophagy, ROS detoxification | Orthology and expression support, ideally tissue-aware | Cold/deep-sea metabolic adaptation can mimic longevity hypotheses |
| Immune and inflammatory regulation | `TLR`, `TNF`, NF-kB-related candidates, cytokine signaling | Gene-family and domain support with copy-number artifact checks | Immune adaptation is not aging-specific without functional evidence |
| Proteostasis and autophagy | Chaperones, ubiquitin-proteasome, lysosome, ER stress genes | Orthology-supported copy number and expression support | Ubiquitous stress pathways have broad ecological roles |
| Retinal preservation | Phototransduction, retinal maintenance, DNA repair in retina | Tissue-specific RNA-seq and histology where public metadata support it | Retinal findings are not whole-organism proof |

## Current Orthology Status

The first real OrthoFinder run was executed externally in Docker and postprocessed by the repository. Current candidate tables use exact GFF gene symbols/compact aliases and collapse protein isoforms to unique Greenland shark loci. These outputs help prioritize follow-up, but they are not final biological conclusions.

Current high-priority unresolved cases include `H1F0`, `FTH1B`, `TP53`, and `RAD51` under exact-symbol mapping. These should be treated as annotation uncertainty, not gene absence. The repository now prepares a high-priority rescue table for these genes and exports representative proteins for mapped candidates.

The implemented domain-validation path is HMMER/Pfam domain checking on representative candidate proteins. The first run classified the 24 mapped representative candidates as `DOMAIN_SUPPORTED`, which supports protein-model plausibility for those mapped representatives. It does not establish pathway activity, protein function in vivo, validated copy-number change, or longevity relevance.

The first Phase 4 annotation-rescue path now applies reciprocal protein similarity to unresolved high-priority candidates. It produced protein-level rescue candidates for `H1F0`, `FTH1B`, and `RAD51`; `TP53` remains unresolved under the configured reciprocal-protein filters. These findings are follow-up targets, not final annotations.

Phase 4b now runs a limited candidate-scaffold miniprot genome-validation gate for these targets. The current run reports separable high-coverage candidate-scaffold alignments for `H1F0`, `FTH1B`, and `RAD51`; `TP53` remains unresolved in the protein-rescue profile and is handled separately by a bounded targeted p53-family search. This preserves the hypothesis structure without weakening the evidence standard: no loss, validated duplication, pathway activity, or mechanism-level interpretation is made from these genome-alignment inputs without manual locus review and cross-resource support.

Phase 4d consolidates the current validation status. `H1F0`, `FTH1B`, and `RAD51` are Tier 2 candidate-locus leads because they have high-coverage miniprot support and Pfam domain support, but their duplication status is not validated. `FTH1B` remains especially artifact-prone because ferritin-family loci, isoforms, fragments, and local genomic context must be separated manually. `TP53` remains `Artifact/uncertain`; the targeted p53-family alignment is a candidate locus requiring manual exon, domain, and orthology review, not a functional or gene-state conclusion.

Phase 4e hardens the manual-review record by comparing candidate loci with the local Figshare annotation GFF. The current hardened summary keeps `H1F0` and `RAD51` as Tier 2 candidate loci requiring cross-resource validation. `FTH1B` remains Tier 2 but high artifact risk: 12 loci are domain-supported, 11 overlap ferritin-family annotation, 8 overlap the exact/focal annotation model that supplied the rescue protein, and 1 carries a miniprot disruption flag. `TP53` remains `Artifact/uncertain` because the targeted p53-family alignment is product-consistent but nonfocal and carries a miniprot disruption flag.

Phase 5 adds limited repeat-context hardening for the same high-priority loci. The Tokyo/PNAS/Figshare package did not provide a parseable repeat annotation, so Phase 5b used an external Docker/Linux RepeatModeler2 plus RepeatMasker route to create a candidate-locus repeat-context input. The current bounded Phase 5 table records direct repeat overlap for the `FTH1B` cluster, `RAD51` loci, and the `TP53` targeted p53-family locus; `H1F0` has repeat annotations in the local window but no direct overlap. These observations are artifact/context evidence only, not transposable-element mechanism or longevity evidence.

Phase 5b records the optional de novo repeat-annotation route using RepeatModeler2 and RepeatMasker in Docker or WSL/Linux. It can overcome the missing-repeat-annotation limitation for local context review, but the current RepeatMasker run did not produce a `.tbl` summary and includes a `ProcessRepeats` signal-9 warning. Repeat overlap therefore remains artifact/context evidence unless independent locus, annotation, repeat-class, and cross-resource validation support a stronger interpretation.

Phase 5c hardens this repeat-context layer by comparing the GFF-derived overlap calls with a window-filtered parse of the RepeatMasker `.out` file. This supports the repeat-overlap artifact-risk flags for `FTH1B`, `RAD51`, and `TP53`, and keeps `H1F0` as local-window context without direct overlap. The result improves artifact-risk handling for future Phase 8/Phase 9 evidence scoring, but it still does not make a repeat-mediated biological mechanism claim.

Phase 6 adds telomere-related sequence context and gene-readiness outputs. The current exact-motif scan of the Tokyo assembly records enrichment of canonical `TTAGGG`/`CCCTAA` motifs in scaffold-end windows relative to adjacent internal control windows, and the current telomere/shelterin gene audit maps `TERT`, `TERF1`, `TERF2`, `POT1`, `TINF2`, `ACD`, and `TERF2IP` as single-copy exact-symbol candidates in the post-OrthoFinder table. This is useful readiness evidence for future validation, but it does not support telomere length, telomerase activity, rejuvenation, pathway activity, or longevity-mechanism claims.

Phase 7a registers RNA-seq metadata from `PRJNA1246101` for future tissue-specific expression support. Three PolyA paired-end retinal RNA-seq runs are metadata-ready for a future candidate expression audit, while the WGS run is excluded from expression planning. This strengthens the readiness layer for retinal preservation and retina-relevant DNA repair hypotheses, but it does not yet show that any candidate is detected or expressed. Future expression results must remain retina-specific unless broader tissue data are added.

Phase 7b defines how candidate expression should be quantified later and which candidate identifiers are safe to summarize. The current strategy recommends Salmon-style transcript quantification from an annotation-derived transcript FASTA for the first pass, with genome-aligned counting reserved for paralogous or repeat-sensitive candidates such as `FTH1B`, `H1F0`, `RAD51`, and `TP53`. This improves the future expression-support layer for retinal preservation and candidate maintenance pathways, but it remains a planning/readiness step. It does not provide expression detection, differential expression, pathway activity, or longevity-mechanism evidence.

Phase 7c constructs the transcript reference and validates candidate gene IDs before any raw-read analysis. This is a necessary control for future expression work because incorrect transcript-to-gene mapping can make TPM/count tables look precise while summarizing the wrong isoforms, paralogs, or unresolved loci. The current Phase 7c layer therefore strengthens future retinal expression analysis design, but it still does not provide expression evidence.

Phase 7d adds the first retina-only raw-read processing gate. It records whether local paired FASTQs are available, runs lightweight FASTQ sanity QC, checks Salmon/index readiness, and summarizes candidate TPM/read counts only after successful Salmon quantification. These outputs can support cautious `detected in retina` or `not detected under these conditions` language for candidates with clear transcript mapping. They do not support differential expression, pathway activity, whole-organism aging interpretation, or longevity-mechanism claims. High-ambiguity candidates still require locus-aware review before expression support is used in integrated evidence scoring.

Phase 7e and Phase 8a harden that expression layer before it affects evidence scoring. Phase 7e reviews mapping rates, transcript-reference ambiguity, duplicated or repeat-context loci, and candidate-level quantification caveats. Phase 8a then carries only the hardened retina-specific expression context into evidence tables while preserving existing biological evidence tiers. This means expression support can improve interpretability for low-ambiguity candidates, but it does not turn a candidate into a validated longevity mechanism or rescue unresolved loci such as `FTH1B`, `H1F0`, `RAD51`, or `TP53`.

Phase 8b converts the accumulated evidence into final pre-reporting classes. The current audit leaves 31 candidates as Tier 2 plausible leads and 10 as `Artifact/uncertain`; none meet Tier 1 criteria. `FTH1B`, `H1F0`, and `RAD51` remain plausible but artifact-prone because locus, paralog, repeat-context, and cross-resource validation are still unresolved. `TP53` remains uncertain. These classifications are useful for prioritizing Phase 9 reporting and future validation, but they are not mechanism claims.
